UX with the Fam: Clean vs Clear

UX with the Fam will be an ongoing series that relates stories about UX (User Experience).

A few weeks ago my mom asked me how to take a picture using her phone. She had recently upgraded from a phone that ran Android 4.0 with Sony’s custom UI to a phone that ran stock Android 4.4. The image below depicts the interface that she saw on her new phone. She managed to take videos, but wasn’t able to take a picture.

My mom’s previous phone’s camera interface had an icon depicting a camera, similar to the one on the bottom right of the above image, but without the circular arrow. She knew on her old phone that when she pressed that button it took a picture. Not having this same icon on her new phone, she switched between pressing the bottom right button (which toggled the camera being used from rear-facing to front-facing) and the top left button (which started recording video).

I explained that she could take a picture by tapping anywhere on the screen, except on the icons. Not really intuitive.

How was she suppose to know? I think the very first time the camera app is launched it provides an overlay of instructions explaining that you tap the screen to snap a picture. The problem is that if you don’t initially use the camera app enough to imprint the actions needed, you may forget and resort to your previous mental model. It’s a case of trying to remember vs recognize. Remembering is much harder!

Trading clear signifiers for a “cleaner” design is generally not a good idea. Good design should be aesthetic and unobtrusive, but not at the expense of being understandable.

Note: It appears that Android 5.0 does have a signifier (the camera icon) for taking a pictures within the camera app. At least this is the case with my Dad’s Nexus 5.
