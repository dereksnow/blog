Vim and the Art of Text Manipulation

Last year (2014) I was looking to increase my text editing productivity and had the time to take the upfront productivity hit to learn Emacs or Vim. Initially I started learning Emacs since it integrates well with existing Unix tools and I could basically “live” in Emacs for most things… However, I really dislike the use of key chords for most operations. If you’re not familiar with key chords, it simply means holding one or more keys down while pressing another( e.g Ctrl+C, Alt+F4). So, I turned to Vim, which takes a modal approach (i.e. switching between different modes: normal, insert, visual, command etc to accomplish tasks). Vim’s modal approach employees single key and key sequences to perform operations, instead of key chords. This proved to be a much better approach for me and my inflexible fingers.

Note: Emacs has packages (e.g evil) that support Vim keybindings. I may move to this type of setup at some point…

So is Learning Vim (or Emacs) really worth it?

Someone who spends at least 30 - 40 diliberate hours of learning/practicing Vim (or Emacs) on average will be significantly more productive at text editing. So, if you spend a lot time editing, you should at least give it a try. You can take incremental steps so as not to completeley tank your productivity.

Learning Vim

I was lucky enough to find a great piece of “edutainment” software for learning Vim last year - Vim Adventures (VA). VA describes itself as the “Zelda meets text editing” game. Comparisons to Zelda are probably a bit much, but Vim Adventures is a great way to learn the basics of Vim. The game engages you in a quest that has you repeating more than 60 Vim commands and motions that build up your muscle memory very fast. I felt significantly more productive using Vim after a week of playing VA.

  2. Vim Adventures
  3. Seven habits of effective text editing - Tips from the Vim creator himself
  4. Learn to speak vim - verbs, nouns, and modifiers!

Other editors

There are a lot of text editors available besides Vim and Emacs (yeah, Emacs is more of an OS than just a text editor) that allow people to be very productive. The important thing is to learn your editor and become proficient in using it. If you think it’s lacking in features, you may want to explore other options.
