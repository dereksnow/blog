UX with the Fam - Part 1

UX with the Fam will be an ongoing series that relates stories about UX (User
Experience).

The Issue

My dad has had a Nexus 5 since it came out in 2013. He mostly uses it to talk
and text, but he also enjoys using Google Now to get information. A few weeks
ago he asked me why his phone no longer audibly spelled-out words that he asked
it to spell. He demonstrated what was happening - OK Google, spell embarrass.
Google returned search results for spell embarrass, with the first result
being merriam-webster’s entry for embarrass. Just as he said, there wasn’t an
audible spelling of embarrass as he expected.

What was happening?

I first assumed that something had changed with his recent upgrade to Android
5.0. I decided to try out OK Google, spell embarrass on my phone, since it was
still at 4.4. My phone returned the same merriam-webster entry without the word
embarrass audibly spelled. Hmm… maybe spell wasn’t the right thing to say
and I tried OK Google, how do you spell embarrass? - which did result in the
word being spelled out. I must have used a similar phrase when I first showed
him this feature. It seems we had both forgot the magic phrase…

User Expectations

My dad expected to hear the word spelled-out, but didn’t use the “correct”
phrase. He assumed it didn’t matter which phrase was used as long as the same
basic meaning was conveyed. This is a reasonable expectation. I explained that
natural language processing(NLP) is not trivial and it’s very impressive what
Google and other companies have accomplished with speech recognition and NLP.
With that said, I don’t think this was case of being technically hard, but more
likely an oversight, side effect or possibly intentional. After all, Google was
returning a dictionary entry for embarrass when spell embarrass was used,
not something unrelated. It should also be noted that how do you spell… is
not the only phrase that can be used to get an audible spelling of a word, give
me the spelling for the word… also results in the word being audibly spelled.

A better UX?

Providing consistent and predictable behavior is part of good user centered
design. Predictable behavior results in the user being satisfied that the
product/service is working reliably. Inconsistent behavior delays the user from
their intended goal, which can be frustrating.

So providing a consistent experience for semantically similar queries (e.g.
spell embarrass, how do you spell embarrass) is ideal. Realizing NLP isn’t
perfect, Google and other companies using voice recognition/NLP could allow
users to opt-in to a program to help identify semantically similar phrases. As
an example, after saying OK Google, spell embarrass and providing the result,
it could ask does “spell embarrass” have the same meaning as “how do you spell
embarrass”. With enough responses in the affirmative, the phrase could be
grouped with other phrases as being semantically equivalent and provide
consistent behavior.
